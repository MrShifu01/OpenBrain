Everion vs 1Password

Last updated: 2026-04-29 · 5 min read

Short answer: Use both. 1Password is built for credentials — usernames, passwords, 2FA codes, SSH keys. Everion is built for the free-form facts that aren't credentials: gate codes, policy numbers, IDs, medical aid numbers, the doctor's contact, "if I die" notes for your spouse. They don't compete. Storing one in the other is a category mistake.

Why this comparison comes up

People reach for a password manager when they want a "secure place to store private information." Then they try to put a gate code or a policy number in there and the UI starts to feel wrong — secure notes get buried, search is by title not meaning, and there's no chat-style recall. They didn't need a password manager for that fact. They needed a vault built for free-form content.

What 1Password does that Everion doesn't

  • Browser autofill for logins. 1Password's whole reason to exist. Everion has no autofill.
  • 2FA / TOTP codes. Built into 1Password. Not in Everion.
  • SSH key management. 1Password has dedicated developer features.
  • Long industry record on credentials. Audited, watch-towered for breaches, the benchmark for password storage.
  • Family plans with granular sharing. 1Password's family vault model is more developed than Everion's one-other-person sharing.

What Everion does that 1Password doesn't

  • Free-form capture. The same atomic entry holds a half-thought, a voice memo, a screenshot, or a serial number. No item-type form to fill out.
  • Recall by asking. "When does my licence expire" works. "What's the policy number for the car" works. "Where did I hide the spare key" works. AI cites source entries.
  • "If I die" notes for non-credential life facts. The where-the-deeds-live, the executor's contact, the funeral wishes, the "tell my spouse this." A password manager isn't shaped for prose.
  • Shared brain that includes context, not just shared logins. The Pro shared-brain holds the gate code and the half-thought you had about the gate-code keypad. Sharing on 1Password is shared logins.
  • BYO AI key. On the free Hobby tier, prompts go directly to your provider — your own Anthropic / OpenAI / OpenRouter / Groq key. Nothing routes through Everion's servers.

The right combination

1Password for everything you log into. Everion for everything you'd remember about your life if you wrote it all down. Together they cover the full surface of "things I need to keep private" — credentials in 1Password, free-form in Everion. Most Everion users keep their password manager. We expect that. They're complementary, not substitutes.

FAQ

Should I use 1Password or Everion?

Use both. They cover different ground. 1Password is built for credentials — usernames, passwords, 2FA codes, SSH keys. Everion is built for the free-form facts that aren't credentials — gate codes, policy numbers, ID and bank details, medical aid numbers, "where I hid the spare key", "what does my spouse need to know if something happens to me". Storing free-form notes in 1Password is uncomfortable; storing credentials in Everion is the wrong tool.

Can 1Password store gate codes and policy numbers?

Technically yes — 1Password has a "secure note" item type. In practice the UI is wrong for free-form facts: there's no AI search, no chat-style recall, and notes get buried. Everion is built around free-form capture and recall by asking.

Is Everion as secure as 1Password?

Both use end-to-end encryption for high-stakes content. 1Password has been audited extensively for credential storage and is the long-standing benchmark for password managers. Everion is local-first with an end-to-end encrypted vault layer for high-stakes facts; encryption details are documented in the privacy policy. Use 1Password for credentials. Use Everion for everything else worth keeping.

Does Everion replace 1Password?

No. Everion does not store credentials. It stores notes, half-thoughts, and high-stakes facts (gate codes, account numbers, IDs, medical info, "if I die" notes). Keep 1Password for usernames and passwords. Add Everion for the free-form layer that doesn't fit a credential manager.

Can Everion share with my family the way 1Password does?

Everion's Pro tier supports a shared brain with one other person — typically a spouse or next-of-kin. It's narrower than 1Password's family plans on purpose: the shared layer is for the things that matter (gate code, policy number, "if something happens to me" notes), not a workspace for the whole household.
