The State of the Second Brain, 2026

Last updated: 2026-04-29 · 10 min read · A research synthesis

"Second brain" started as a productivity-blog phrase. It's become a real software category because the underlying problem — too much information, scattered across too many tools, retrievable only by the person who put it there — keeps getting worse. This page synthesises what the research actually says about that problem, with sources you can verify. We'll refresh it quarterly and add Everion's own usage data once we have a meaningful sample.

The information overload baseline

The most-cited number on knowledge-worker time loss comes from McKinsey Global Institute's The Social Economy study (2012). It found that knowledge workers spend nearly two hours of every working day searching for or gathering information they need to do their jobs:

19%

of the average knowledge worker's week is spent searching for and gathering information — roughly 9.3 hours.

Source: McKinsey Global Institute, The Social Economy: Unlocking value and productivity through social technologies (2012). Read the original.

The number is old, but it has been repeatedly re-confirmed in subsequent research. Microsoft's Work Trend Index reports have tracked the same kind of overload year after year:

"The pace, complexity, and volume of work has increased to the point that 68% of people don't have enough uninterrupted focus time during the workday."

Source: Microsoft, Work Trend Index Annual Report (2023). Read the report.

Translation: the median knowledge worker spends a full working day per week looking for things they already have, and never gets enough quiet time to think about any of it. The cost is real and measurable.

App sprawl: the structural reason

Information is hard to find because it's spread across more tools than any single person can keep in their head. Industry research consistently shows the average knowledge worker now toggles between roughly a dozen apps every working day:

~10

apps an average knowledge worker uses, with ~9 daily and 1,200+ context-switches per day across them.

Source: Harvard Business Review summarising research from a leading workflow vendor — "How Much Time and Energy Do We Waste Toggling Between Applications?" (HBR, 2022). Read the article.

Each toggle costs attention. The HBR analysis estimated workers lose around 4 hours a week just regaining context after switching applications. That's about 5 working weeks a year evaporated to "wait, where was I?"

Why notes apps don't solve it on their own

The category response has been notes apps with AI bolted on. The problem is that capture friction — the work of choosing a folder, picking a template, naming the page — is what kills most second-brain experiments. The Notion / Obsidian / Roam ecosystem is wide and demanding; abandonment is high once setup work outpaces value.

The behavioural pattern is well-known: most second-brain failures happen at the front door, not at the back end. If capture takes more than a few seconds, people stop. If retrieval requires recalling where you filed something, people stop. If the system has to be designed before it can be used, people stop.

The vault problem nobody owns

There's a parallel category most second-brain conversations skip: the high-stakes facts people need but don't know where to keep. Gate codes, policy numbers, ID and bank details, medical aid numbers, "where the spare key is hidden", "what does my spouse need to know if something happens to me."

These don't fit a notes app (the privacy posture is wrong) or a password manager (the UI is wrong — secure notes get buried, there's no chat-style recall, and the format is built for credentials). They typically end up in three places: your head, your spouse's head, or a paper folder nobody can find when it's needed.

Estate-planning research is sparse but consistent: family members often spend weeks or months reconstructing administrative facts after a death, simply because the deceased was the only person who knew where things lived. The cost is administrative time, missed deadlines, and the emotional load on people already grieving.

What changed in 2024–2026: AI search and citations

The biggest 2024–2026 shift in personal knowledge management isn't a new app — it's that retrieval has stopped being keyword-based. The same retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) techniques that power Perplexity and ChatGPT's web search now power "ask my notes" interfaces.

The practical result: "recall by asking" works for the first time at consumer-grade quality. You don't need to remember which folder you filed something in or what title you gave the page. You ask — "when does my driver's licence expire?", "what did the customer push back on last quarter?", "where did I hide the spare key?" — and get an answer with citations to your own past entries.

The two implications for the category:

  • Capture friction matters more, not less. If the AI can find anything, the bottleneck is what you actually captured. Tools with high capture friction (folder pickers, template choices) get less corpus, get worse recall, lose.
  • Privacy posture becomes structural, not optional. If the AI is reading your gate codes and policy numbers, the encryption story has to be exact. Cloud-first tools that "encrypt at rest" without end-to-end encryption are exposed in a way they weren't when notes were just keyword-searched text files.

What people actually need (synthesis)

Pulling the threads together, the 2026 picture looks like this:

  1. One place for both halves — fleeting thoughts AND high-stakes facts. Splitting them across a notes app, a password manager, and "my partner remembers" produces the same retrieval failure mode every time.
  2. Capture in under five seconds. One field. No folder pick. No template. No required title. The format that survives is the one that doesn't ask you to design it.
  3. Recall by asking, not by searching. RAG-grade retrieval with citations to source entries is now table stakes for any tool that wants to be used.
  4. End-to-end encrypted vault for the high-stakes layer. If the tool holds bank details and IDs, "encrypted at rest" is not enough.
  5. An honest free tier. Most personal knowledge management is by-and-for one individual — not a team purchase. Tools that can't be used genuinely free will be replaced by ones that can.
  6. A way for one other person to find what they need. The household / next-of-kin angle is real. Multi-person team plans are the wrong shape; a single shared brain with one trusted person is closer to what people actually want.
